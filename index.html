Where did you start your innovation journey?

01

Ondine:

The fellowship sounded like an exciting adventure, but I didn’t really know what to expect. I already had a project in mind - a community of practice for UNHCR web editors - but I wasn’t really planning on making it my ‘innovation project’ and it took me some time to realize that it could actually be a good option.

01

Helena:

I got an email from a friend about the fellowship. I thought, innovation and UNHCR?, sounds pretty cool! I didn’t think I would get in but I had to try! I had no idea of what to expect from the fellowship. Even Though I’ve always felt close to innovation (due to my work with medical devices), I never saw myself as one. In fact, I thought I was too often playing it safe at work and that this fellowship would give the tools and confidence to be more disruptive.

02

  • Did not know what to expect and what would be the route
  • Pretty disappointing: initially I finish where I started, is where I finished.
  • Learning more about design thinking from UNHCR thinking
  • Videos and hats – insert a collage? A map where we were in the moment of start and where we are now? But place does not matter, it is the route that matters?

Where are you now?

03

Ondine:

the Community of Practice will actually be launched tomorrow, and it’s funny to see how its development has followed the tempo of the fellowship itself.

Helena:

my project didn’t end up being a punctual project that will end with my fellowship. I see it more as an ongoing transformation on how I think about problems I encounter, on the approach that I take to solve them, and how it’s good to question the status quo a little more often than I used too.

ALL:

Different stages: moving with the project, feeling bad about not moving forward with it. Learnt a lot and more experienced.

What’s different?

04

  • The route has been different than what I initially anticipated: not direct, but rather a long and winding road. I was initially focusing on the implementation only, but the fellowship forced me to step back to self-reflect, take some detours to get feedback from a lot of different colleagues, and question the utility and usability of the product at every step. In the end, I think (and hope) that I get a much better product than what I would have delivered without the fellowship.
  • Things changed. World has changed since. We have changed in how we think, what we do and how we approach things.
  • I didn’t know what to expect. I had some preconception on how things would be and what I’d do. I thought I’d had this one big project and at the end I got many smaller projects but I think it turned out better and more interesting than I imagined it.

Where do you go next?

05

Ondine: Even if the community is live, it is still work in progress. The idea is to involve web editors as much as possible in the improvements and the support to each other. So, I see the community like a live organism which is about to grow, mutate, be sick, be better. And probably die at some point, since a new and better solution will come up.

Helena: I’d like to keep the innovation flame burning within me. Not lose the motivation. I’d like to be able to implement what I’ve learnt so far in my job and in any other projects where I get involved; develop more skills and knowledge in this area.

Is there enlightenment?

07

Yes and no

08

Yes, a bit.

09

I don’t feel ‘enlightened’ as such, but the fellowship has been an opportunity to shine light on some key concepts which are key to see things from a different angle and build a more effective collaboration.

Should there be enlightenment?

10

It does not require it, it brings it. We can do things differently.

What is the enlightenment for you (me)?

  • The enlightenment for me has been self-consciousness. I am more aware of the way I work, of how other people do, and of the world of possibilities still to explore.
  • There was enlightenment to shed the light on those dark corners of collaboration, group work, moving forward.
  • Things one change and improve – was not a 180-degree change. And eye opener. It does not need to be WOW, it is rather little moments that build up the path to WOW.
  • The best thing that happened was not to do the project, it helped to get involved in different activities in the office other than daily work. I am going to have my WOW moment soon.

For the support team and the people you have worked with (we)?

We inter-influenced each other. Collaboration, diversity, enrichment. Enriching each other. It helped to do individual work, and for the people we work with – it is not necessarily that we enlightened them. User centric approach is quite accepted in the digital world (Ondine). Cynical the older we are – so maybe colleagues in our offices did not show as much interest as we would have expected/hoped. Two main people left. Project fall and begin. Continuation is a challenge.

For the organisation (us)?

We need enlightenment. Traditional way of working, and many are not responsive to change. People do not like change. Constantly unmotivated because of discouraging comments about being young and energetic. It is hard to push a new idea, there is a feeling of huge resistance. So it is necessary. We are going there, slowly. Culture change.

What happens next?

11

The community of practice is meant to continue, evolve, adapt and grow. I feel like a Dr Frankenstein who wants to keep experimenting with the living thing he has created. Success is not necessary. It does not matter if the project is implemented and adopted, it is the journey that matters. It is about learning about innovation and about yourself. Keep enlightenment alive. Keep learning alive. Not to go back to “traditional” or how I was doing it before. I do not want the fellowship to end. What is after?
